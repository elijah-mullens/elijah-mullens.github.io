Elijah Mullens

I am a fourth-year PhD student at Cornell University's Department of Astronomy interested in the characterization of exoplanets via atmospheric retrievals, laboratory work, and forward models.

My main interest is exploring silicate clouds in hot exoplanet atmospheres by taking laboratory measurements of optical properties of silicates, building forward chemical and global climate models, and fitting JWST data with atmospheric retrievals. My favorite planets are WD 1856b, WASP-17b, and Neptune.

I grew up in the panhandle of Florida on the 'Forgotten Coast' and received a B.S. in Mathematics, Physics, and Astrophysics (with a minor in Japanese) from the University of Florida in 2021, completing a portion of my degree in an exchange program at Nagoya University (名古屋大学). During my gap year, between undergraduate and graduate school, I worked as a planetarium instructor where I gave talks and demonstrations to young students and the general public on topics ranging from local astronomy to the basics of general relativity. I recieved my M.S. from Cornell University in 2024.

All of the code I develop for my research is open-source and linked below, with multiple user-friendly tutorials. Please feel free to reach out if you have any questions about my projects or open-source codes below, or if you want to collaborate!

Research Highlights

Atmospheric Retrievals with Mie Scattering Aerosols

I was the lead developer of POSEIDON v1.2, which included Mie-scattering aerosols in transmission, thermal emission, and reflection geometries. POSEIDON is an open-source atmospheric retrieval code developed by Dr. Ryan MacDonald.

Implementation of Aerosol Mie Scattering in POSEIDON with Application to the Hot Jupiter HD 189733 b's Transmission, Emission, and Reflected Light Spectrum (Mullens et al. 2024)

POSEIDON GitHub

Crystalline Grain Directionality

I explored the observable effects of crystalline grain directionality in hot Jupiter atmospheres, utilizing the orientation-specific attenuation to probe for aerosol-wind interactions.

The same physical processes that produce beautiful optical phenomena such as sun dogs, crown flashes, 'fire rainbows', and moon halos can be detected in mid-infrared wavelengths to probe aerosol-atmospheric interactions. Alongside the paper below POSEIDON v1.3.1 was released, which I was the lead developer on.

Silicate Sundogs: Probing the Effects of Grain Directionality in Exoplanet Observations (Mullens & Lewis 2025)



WASP-17b JWST Observations

I am the retrieval lead for the WASP-17b transmission analyses part of the Deep Reconnaissance of Exoplanet Atmospheres through Multi-instrument Spectroscopy (DREAMS) program where POSEIDON was utilized to detect SiO2 crystals in the atmosphere of WASP-17b.

JWST-TST DREAMS: Quartz Clouds in the Atmosphere of WASP-17b (Grant et al. 2023)

JWST-TST DREAMS: A Precise Water Abundance for Hot Jupiter WASP-17b from the NIRISS SOSS Transmission Spectrum (Louie et al. 2025)

I am the PI for JWST Cycle 4 GO 8309: It’s (poly)Morphin’ Time! Solving the quartz quandary of WASP-17b, with observations slated to take place in 2026.

Eccentric Objects

Eccentric objects provide a nature-given laboratory to explore how atmospheric chemistry, cloud formation/sublimation, and dynamics react to changing incident stellar radiation. These objects also experience tidal heating that allow us to probe the interior structure of these objects.

My current research focuses on exploring eccentric transiting brown dwarfs and sub-Neptunes.

I am the PI for JWST Cycle 4 GO 7686: Brown Dwarf Broiler: Probing Chemical Quenching and Heat Redistribution in a Highly-Eccentric Brown Dwarf, with observations slated to take place in 2026.

Post Main Sequence

Most stars with exoplanets will evolve through the red giant branch and end up as a white dwarf. I am interested in exploring the surface and atmospheric evolution of objects during and after the post main sequence by forward modeling of Solar System objects and fitting JWST spectra of exoplanets around white dwarfs.

I developed a code to model the 2D surface evolution of the icy moon Europa during the red giant branch to study if it can retain its surface water during and after the post main sequence.

Life after death: Europa in the evolving Habitable Zone of a Red Sun (Mullens et al. 2024)

ISM Simulation Analysis

I developed a pipeline that automatically fits 3-dimension radial density profiles of molecular clouds in ISM observations and simulations.

Characterizing the 3D Structure of Molecular Cloud Envelopes in the Cloud Factory Simulations (Mullens et al. 2024)
